\section{Motivation}
% Introduction goes here
Reliable task execution remains an open problem for humanoid robots. Kicking a football requires well-coordinated kinematics to cover approach, stance, hip rotation, leg swing, balance, and other nuances, but it does have a clear success metric. We would like to explore how much SONIC's pre-trained whole-body prior improves policy performance and reduces the cost of learning this skill, both in simulation and in real life. Also, it would be pretty cool to teach a robot some football.

\section{Milestones}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Demo Collection and Reward Design:} Record a human kicking demo, extract leg swing trajectory, ball contact region, and other key features. Define a reward function and postural naturalness term from the SONIC prior.
    \item \textbf{Sim Policy Training:} RL fine-tune SONIC on the kicking task in sim. Compare SONIC-initialised vs. scratch-trained policy on sample efficiency and performance.
    \item \textbf{Sim Robustness:} Introduce domain randomisations over key variables and measure policy success rate across trials.
    \item \textbf{Real Robot Deployment:} Deploy the best sim policy on the physical G1. Measure success rate and document failures.
    \item \textbf{Real World Fine-tuning:} Fine-tune the policy on real hardware to close the sim-to-real gap.
\end{itemize}

\section{Risks & Fallbacks}
The primary risk is sim-to-real transfer, as contact-rich tasks are sensitive to environmental variables like ground friction and joint compliance, which can cause the policy to behave other than expected. We mitigate this through comprehensive testing during the robustness phase. If transfer remains difficult, we will attempt sim-to-sim validation in a second simulator such as MuJoCo to attempt and isolate if causes may be simulator related. In the worst case, simulation-only results including the SONIC-vs-scratch ablation remain a self-contained and meaningful contribution.
